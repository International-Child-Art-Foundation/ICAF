                                    Movement is ExistenceDr. Arnold B. Udoka talks to ChildArt13January - March 2024after, I started consulting for the government and received a presidential appointment as the national choreographer and director of dance. While in that position, I attended the International Choreographers Residency Program of the American Dance Festival at Duke University, Durham. I also returned to my studies at the University of Calabar. I obtained a doctorate in Directing with a dissertation on Mythic Imagery in Indigenous Nigerian Dance-Drama: A Directorial Approach Towards Contemporary Theatre Practice.As a former student of the priesthood, I believe that dance is important because movement is the ingredient of life; without movement, there is no dance. As the Book of Genesis says, “In the beginning, God created the heavens and earth, the earth had no form and was void; without form;  darkness covered the deep, while the spirit of God hovered over the water” (Gen. 1:1-2). The hovering spirit signifies that movement is the first element of life. Everything in the human body must move, consciously or unconsciously, voluntarily or involuntarily. Without movement, man does not exist. Movement is also for communication, and each culture has its own language. The idea of dance is to help human beings communicate. In Nigeria, we have more than 600 different ethnic groups, meaning there are 600 methods of communication through movement and dance. To unite the Nigerian nation, people must have the opportunity to develop and appreciate their cultures. Culture is colonized in Nigeria. We need a deliberate effort to make people understand their cultures instead of eroding or imposing their cultures on other cultures, as has happened several times in Nigeria. When colonization exists, national unity is just a dream. Dance has done and can do more for our society than the entertainment that colonial and commercial forces have reduced it to.Dr. Arnold B. Udoka was the national choreographer of the National Troupe of Nigeria. He has received several awards for his choreographies and for promoting Nigerian heritage. He is married to Pamela, a clinical psychologist and theatre artist. They have three adorable children.
